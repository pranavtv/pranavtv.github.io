Section 1 · Foundation

What is Statistics?

Statistics is the science of learning from data — collecting, organizing, analyzing, interpreting, and presenting information to make decisions under uncertainty.

🎯
The Core Definition
Statistics transforms raw numbers into meaningful understanding. It is the mathematical language of uncertainty, giving us tools to reason from incomplete information to confident conclusions.
Science of DataUncertainty
🌍
Why Statistics Exists
The world generates more data than any human can manually process. We need systematic tools to detect patterns, test theories, and make predictions — statistics provides exactly these tools.
Decision MakingPattern Detection
⚖️
Uncertainty & Decisions
Every decision made without complete information involves uncertainty. Statistics quantifies that uncertainty, turning vague guesses into calibrated probabilities and rigorous inference.
ProbabilityInference
📈
Data → Knowledge Pipeline
Statistics defines the pipeline: collect data → clean and organize → describe and visualize → model and infer → interpret and communicate → decide and act.
PipelineWorkflow
🔬
Statistics vs Mathematics
Mathematics deals with certainty and proof. Statistics deals with data and uncertainty. Statistics uses mathematical tools — but its questions are empirical, not abstract.
EmpiricalApplied Math
🤝
Statistics & Science
Modern science is fundamentally statistical. Clinical trials, particle physics experiments, climate models, economic forecasts — all rely on statistical inference to validate discoveries.
Scientific MethodValidation

The Two Pillars of Statistics

📊 Descriptive Statistics

Summarizes and describes the data you have. Answers: "What does this data look like?" Uses means, medians, charts, and graphs to paint a picture of the dataset.

Mean = (Σxᵢ) / n

🔭 Inferential Statistics

Draws conclusions about a population from a sample. Answers: "What can I conclude beyond my data?" Uses hypothesis tests, confidence intervals, and probability models.

P(reject H₀ | H₀ true) = α

📐 Measures of Central Tendency

μ Mean (Average) Sum divided by count ▼

Definition

The arithmetic mean is the sum of all values divided by the number of observations. The most common measure of center.

x̄ = (x₁ + x₂ + ... + xₙ) / n = Σxᵢ / n
Example: Scores: 70, 80, 90, 100 → Mean = (70+80+90+100)/4 = 85

Caution

Sensitive to outliers. One extreme value can pull the mean far from the "typical" value.

Geometric Mean Harmonic Mean Weighted Mean
M Median Middle value when sorted ▼

Definition

The median is the middle value when data is sorted. For even n, it's the average of the two middle values. Resistant to outliers.

If n odd: M = x₍ₙ₊₁₎/₂ If n even: M = (x_{n/2} + x_{n/2+1}) / 2
Example: Data: 1, 2, 3, 100 → Mean=26.5, Median=2.5. Median better reflects center.
Quartiles Percentiles Robust Statistics
Mo Mode Most frequent value ▼

Definition

The most frequently occurring value(s) in a dataset. A dataset can be unimodal, bimodal, or multimodal. The only measure that works for categorical data.

Example: Favorite colors: Blue, Red, Blue, Green, Blue → Mode = Blue
Nominal Data Frequency Distribution
Gm Geometric Mean nth root of the product ▼

Definition

Used for growth rates, ratios, and multiplicative data. Always less than or equal to the arithmetic mean.

G = (x₁ · x₂ · ... · xₙ)^(1/n) = exp(Σ ln(xᵢ) / n)
Example: Investment returns 10%, 20%, 30% → G = (1.1·1.2·1.3)^(1/3) - 1 ≈ 19.7%
Finance Growth Rates

📏 Measures of Dispersion

σ² Variance Average squared deviation ▼

Definition

Variance measures how spread out data is from the mean. Squaring the deviations makes all terms positive.

Population: σ² = Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / N Sample: s² = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n−1)
Note: Sample variance divides by (n−1), not n — this is Bessel's correction for unbiasedness.
Standard DeviationBessel's Correction
σ Standard Deviation √Variance — in original units ▼

Definition

The square root of variance. Expressed in the same units as the data, making it interpretable. The most widely used dispersion measure.

σ = √(σ²) = √(Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / N)
68–95–99.7 Rule: For normal data, ~68% within 1σ, ~95% within 2σ, ~99.7% within 3σ.
Normal DistributionZ-score
IQR Interquartile Range Q3 − Q1 ▼

Definition

The range of the middle 50% of the data. Robust to outliers. Used in boxplots and for detecting outliers (Tukey fences).

IQR = Q3 − Q1 Outlier fences: [Q1 − 1.5·IQR, Q3 + 1.5·IQR]
Example: Data: 1,4,7,10,100 → Q1=4, Q3=10, IQR=6. Value 100 is far outside fences.
BoxplotOutlier DetectionRobust Stats
CV Coefficient of Variation Relative measure of spread ▼

Definition

The ratio of standard deviation to the mean, expressed as a percentage. Allows comparison of variability across datasets with different units or scales.

CV = (σ / μ) × 100%
Example: Heights (CV=4%) vs Weights (CV=15%) → Weights are relatively more variable.
Comparing DatasetsRisk Analysis

🔷 Shape Measures

↗️ Skewness Asymmetry of distribution ▼

Definition

Measures the asymmetry of the probability distribution. Positive skew = long right tail (mean > median). Negative skew = long left tail (mean < median).

Skewness = E[(X − μ)³] / σ³
Income distributions are typically positively skewed — most people earn near the median, but very high earners pull the mean right.
KurtosisNormality Tests
⛰️ Kurtosis Tail heaviness / peakedness ▼

Definition

Measures the heaviness of tails relative to the normal distribution. High kurtosis (leptokurtic) means heavy tails and more extreme outliers.

Kurtosis = E[(X − μ)⁴] / σ⁴ Excess Kurtosis = Kurtosis − 3
Normal distribution has kurtosis = 3 (excess = 0). Heavy-tailed distributions like the t-distribution have excess kurtosis > 0.
Fat TailsRisk ManagementFinance
Section 4 · Inferential Statistics

Drawing Conclusions from Data

Inferential statistics uses sample data to make probabilistic statements about entire populations.

🎲 Probability Theory

Ω Sample Space & Events The foundation of probability ▼

Definitions

Sample Space (Ω): The set of all possible outcomes.
Event (A): A subset of the sample space.
Probability P(A): A number between 0 and 1.

0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1, P(Ω) = 1 P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)
Example: Rolling a die: Ω = {1,2,3,4,5,6}. Event A = "even" = {2,4,6}. P(A) = 3/6 = 0.5
X Random Variables Maps outcomes to numbers ▼

Definition

A random variable assigns a numerical value to each outcome in the sample space. Discrete RVs take countable values; continuous RVs take values in an interval.

Discrete: P(X = x) = p(x) [PMF] Continuous: P(a≤X≤b) = ∫ₐᵇ f(x)dx [PDF]
E[X] = Σ x·P(X=x) for discrete, or ∫ x·f(x)dx for continuous.
PMFPDFCDFExpected Value
P(A|B) Conditional Probability Probability given info ▼

Definition

The probability of event A given that event B has occurred. Fundamental to Bayesian reasoning and many real-world calculations.

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B) Bayes: P(A|B) = P(B|A)·P(A) / P(B)
Medical test: P(Disease | Positive Test) uses Bayes' theorem combining test sensitivity with disease prevalence.
Bayes' TheoremIndependence

🎯 Estimation Theory

θ̂ Maximum Likelihood Estimation Find θ that maximizes L(θ|data) ▼

Definition

MLE finds the parameter values θ that make the observed data most probable. It's the cornerstone of frequentist estimation.

θ̂_MLE = argmax L(θ|x) = argmax ℓ(θ) where ℓ(θ) = log L(θ|x) = Σ log f(xᵢ|θ)
Normal MLE: μ̂ = x̄, σ̂² = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²/n. For large n, MLEs are consistent, efficient, and asymptotically normal.
LikelihoodFisher InformationCramér-Rao Bound
CI Confidence Intervals Range capturing the true parameter ▼

Definition

A 95% CI is constructed so that if repeated many times, 95% of intervals would contain the true parameter. Common misconception: it's NOT a 95% probability the true value is in this specific interval.

95% CI for μ: x̄ ± 1.96 · (σ/√n) or: x̄ ± t_{α/2,n-1} · (s/√n)
Correct interpretation: "We used a method that, in 95% of repetitions, produces an interval containing μ."
t-distributionMargin of ErrorCredible Interval (Bayes)

⚖️ Hypothesis Testing

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

The default assumption. "No effect", "no difference". We test whether data provides enough evidence to reject it.

Type I & II Errors

α = Type I: Reject H₀ when true (false positive)
β = Type II: Fail to reject when false (false negative)
Power = 1 − β

p-value

Probability of observing data as extreme as yours if H₀ were true. NOT the probability H₀ is true. Reject H₀ if p < α.

Section 5 · Mathematical Statistics

The Theoretical Foundations

The rigorous mathematical backbone underlying all of statistical practice — measure theory, decision theory, and asymptotic theory.

📐 Mathematical Prerequisites

Set Theory — Events as sets, probability axioms
Calculus — Integration for probabilities, optimization for MLE
Linear Algebra — Multivariate statistics, covariance matrices
Measure Theory — Rigorous probability foundations
Optimization — Estimation, convex analysis

🔬 Core Theoretical Concepts

Sufficient Statistics ▼

A statistic T(X) is sufficient for θ if the conditional distribution of X given T(X) doesn't depend on θ. It captures all information about θ in the data.

Factorization Theorem: f(x|θ) = g(T(x)|θ)·h(x)
Cramér-Rao Lower Bound ▼

Sets the minimum variance any unbiased estimator can achieve. An estimator achieving this bound is called efficient.

Var(θ̂) ≥ 1/I(θ) where I(θ) = −E[∂²/∂θ² log f(X|θ)]
Central Limit Theorem ▼

The most important theorem in statistics. The sample mean of n i.i.d. random variables approaches normality as n → ∞, regardless of the underlying distribution.

√n(X̄ₙ − μ) → N(0, σ²) as n → ∞
Why it matters: Justifies using normal-distribution-based tests even for non-normal data when n is large.
Law of Large Numbers ▼

As n increases, the sample mean converges to the true population mean. The mathematical guarantee behind all of statistical estimation.

X̄ₙ →ᵖ μ as n → ∞

Section 7 · Regression & Modeling

Regression Analysis

Regression models the relationship between variables — predicting outcomes and understanding how changes in one variable relate to another.

📈 Simple Linear Regression One predictor → one outcome ▼

Model

The simplest regression model relates a continuous outcome Y to one continuous predictor X using a straight line. Estimated by minimizing sum of squared residuals (OLS).

Y = β₀ + β₁X + ε, ε ~ N(0, σ²) β̂₁ = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) / Σ(xᵢ−x̄)², β̂₀ = ȳ − β̂₁x̄
Interpretation: β₁ = "For each 1-unit increase in X, Y changes by β₁ units, on average."

Assumptions (LINE)

Linearity, Independence of errors, Normality of errors, Equal variance (homoscedasticity)

R²Residual AnalysisF-testOLS
📊 Multiple Linear Regression Multiple predictors, matrix form ▼

Model

Extends simple regression to p predictors. OLS solution given in closed form via the normal equations.

Y = β₀ + β₁X₁ + ... + βₚXₚ + ε Matrix form: Y = Xβ + ε OLS: β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹XᵀY
Each βⱼ is the effect of Xⱼ holding all other predictors constant — the partial effect.

Model Quality

R² = proportion of variance explained. Adjusted R² penalizes for extra predictors. Use F-test for overall significance, t-tests for individual coefficients.

MulticollinearityVIFAdjusted R²Heteroscedasticity
🔵 Logistic Regression Binary outcome modeling ▼

Model

Used when outcome is binary (0/1). Models log-odds as linear in predictors. Estimated by Maximum Likelihood, not OLS.

log(p/(1−p)) = β₀ + β₁X₁ + ... + βₚXₚ P(Y=1|X) = 1/(1 + e^{−Xβ})

Interpretation

exp(βⱼ) = odds ratio for a 1-unit increase in Xⱼ. A positive β increases probability of Y=1.

Use: Disease prediction, credit scoring, email spam detection, loan default.
Odds RatioROC CurveAUCGLM
🌀 Regularized Regression (Ridge, LASSO, Elastic Net) Penalized regression for high dimensions ▼

Why Regularize?

When p is large (many predictors), OLS overfits. Regularization adds a penalty on β to shrink coefficients, preventing overfitting and performing implicit variable selection.

Ridge (L2): min ‖Y−Xβ‖² + λΣβⱼ² LASSO (L1): min ‖Y−Xβ‖² + λΣ|βⱼ| Elastic Net: Ridge + LASSO combined

Key Difference

Ridge: Shrinks all coefficients, never to exactly zero.
LASSO: Can set coefficients to exactly zero (automatic variable selection).
Elastic Net: Compromise — good for correlated predictors.

Cross-ValidationBias-Variance TradeoffVariable Selection

Framework

GLMs generalize linear regression to handle non-normal outcomes (counts, binary, positive) through three components: a random component (distribution family), systematic component (linear predictor), and link function.

g(μ) = Xβ where μ = E[Y|X] Link functions: logit, log, identity, inverse

Section 8 · Bayesian Statistics

The Bayesian Paradigm

Bayesian statistics treats probability as a degree of belief, updating that belief as new evidence arrives. A fundamentally different philosophy from frequentist statistics.

Bayes' Theorem — The Core Engine

P(θ|data) ∝ P(data|θ) × P(θ)
📌 P(θ)
Prior
What you believe about θ BEFORE seeing data. Encodes prior knowledge or assumptions.
📊 P(data|θ)
Likelihood
How probable the observed data is, given θ. Comes from your statistical model.
✨ P(θ|data)
Posterior
Updated belief about θ AFTER seeing data. Combines prior knowledge with evidence.
🎯 Prior Distributions Beliefs before data ▼

Types of Priors

Informative prior: Encodes specific prior knowledge. E.g., μ ~ N(70, 10) for human heights.
Weakly informative: Broad priors that regularize without being dogmatic.
Non-informative / flat: Minimal prior information, data drives posterior.
Conjugate prior: Prior and posterior are from the same family (makes math tractable).

Conjugate pairs: Beta(α,β) prior + Binomial likelihood → Beta posterior Normal prior + Normal likelihood → Normal posterior Gamma prior + Poisson likelihood → Gamma posterior
⛓️ MCMC — Markov Chain Monte Carlo Sampling from complex posteriors ▼

The Problem

In most real problems, the posterior P(θ|data) has no analytical form. MCMC generates samples from the posterior by constructing a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is P(θ|data).

Key Algorithms

Metropolis-Hastings: Proposes moves, accepts/rejects based on posterior ratio.
Gibbs Sampling: Samples each parameter from its conditional given others. Efficient when conditionals are known.
Hamiltonian MC (HMC): Uses gradient information for efficient exploration. Used in Stan/PyMC.

Convergence check: R̂ (Gelman-Rubin statistic) ≈ 1.0 indicates chain convergence.
StanPyMCNUTSBurn-in
⚡ Variational Inference Approximate posteriors at scale ▼

Idea

Instead of sampling (MCMC), approximate the posterior with a simpler distribution q(θ) from a tractable family. Minimizes KL divergence KL(q||p).

ELBO = E_q[log p(x,θ)] − E_q[log q(θ)] maximize ELBO ↔ minimize KL(q||p)

Use Cases

Deep learning (VAEs), large-scale Bayesian inference, topic models (LDA). Much faster than MCMC but introduces approximation error.

ELBOMean-Field VIVAEKL Divergence

Section 9 · Time Series Analysis

Analyzing Data Through Time

Time series methods model data collected sequentially in time, capturing patterns like trends, seasonality, and cyclic behavior.

📊 Components of Time Series

📈
Trend (T)
Long-term increase or decrease. GDP growing over decades.
🌀
Seasonality (S)
Regular periodic patterns. Higher retail sales in December.
🔄
Cycles (C)
Longer irregular fluctuations. Business cycles lasting years.
📉
Residual/Noise (ε)
Random variation unexplained by T, S, C.

⚙️ Key Models

AR(p) — Autoregressive:
Xₜ = φ₁Xₜ₋₁ + ... + φₚXₜ₋ₚ + εₜ
MA(q) — Moving Average:
Xₜ = εₜ + θ₁εₜ₋₁ + ... + θqεₜ₋q
ARIMA(p,d,q):
Differenced d times, then ARMA(p,q). Handles non-stationary data.
GARCH — Volatility Clustering:
Models time-varying variance. Standard in financial econometrics.
🏠 Stationarity & ACF/PACF Essential diagnostics for time series ▼

Stationarity

A time series is stationary if its mean, variance, and autocovariance are constant over time. Required for ARMA models. Test with Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test.

ADF test: H₀: series has unit root (non-stationary) Reject H₀ → stationary (safe for ARMA)

ACF & PACF

ACF (Autocorrelation Function): Correlation of series with its own lags. Tells us the MA order q.
PACF (Partial ACF): Correlation after removing intermediate lag effects. Tells us the AR order p.

Section 10 · Multivariate & Survival Analysis

Beyond Single Variables

📐 Multivariate Statistics

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) ▼

Reduces dimensionality by finding orthogonal directions (principal components) that maximize variance. First PC captures the most variance.

Σv = λv (eigenvector decomposition) PC₁ = direction of maximum variance
Use: Face recognition, genome analysis, visualization, noise reduction, preprocessing for ML.
Factor Analysis ▼

Models observed variables as linear combinations of unobserved latent factors plus error. Unlike PCA, factors are not orthogonal and the model has explicit error structure.

X = Λf + ε, where f = latent factors Cov(X) = ΛΛᵀ + Ψ
Use: Psychological testing (IQ, personality), educational assessment, survey analysis.
Cluster Analysis ▼

Finds natural groupings in multivariate data without pre-specified labels. K-means minimizes within-cluster variance; hierarchical clustering builds a dendrogram.

K-means: minimize Σₖ Σᵢ∈Cₖ ‖xᵢ − μₖ‖²
Use: Customer segmentation, image compression, biological taxonomy, market research.

💊 Survival Analysis

Survival & Hazard Functions ▼

S(t) = Survival function: P(survive beyond time t)
h(t) = Hazard function: instantaneous rate of event at time t given survival to t

S(t) = P(T > t) = 1 − F(t) h(t) = f(t) / S(t) = −d/dt[log S(t)]
Kaplan-Meier Estimator ▼

Non-parametric estimator of the survival function. Handles censored observations (when event hasn't occurred by end of study).

Ŝ(t) = ∏ᵢ:tᵢ≤t (1 − dᵢ/nᵢ) where dᵢ = events at tᵢ, nᵢ = at risk
Use: Clinical trials — compare survival curves between treatment groups with log-rank test.
Cox Proportional Hazards Model ▼

Semi-parametric model: leaves baseline hazard unspecified but models covariate effects on hazard. The most widely used survival regression model.

h(t|X) = h₀(t)·exp(β₁X₁ + ... + βₚXₚ) exp(βⱼ) = hazard ratio for Xⱼ
Use: Clinical trials, cancer studies, economics (time-to-event outcomes).
Section 11 · Stochastic Processes & Computational Statistics

Randomness in Time & Computation

🔀 Stochastic Processes

Markov Chains
Future state depends only on present state (not history). P(Xₜ₊₁|Xₜ, Xₜ₋₁,...) = P(Xₜ₊₁|Xₜ). Used in MCMC, NLP, genetics, Google PageRank.
Hidden Markov Models (HMM)
Markov chain with unobserved (hidden) states. Observed outputs depend on hidden state. Speech recognition, NLP, bioinformatics (gene finding).
Brownian Motion / Wiener Process
Continuous-time stochastic process with independent normally distributed increments. Foundation of stochastic calculus, Black-Scholes model in finance.
Poisson Process
Events occur independently at constant rate λ. Arrivals at queues, network packets, earthquake occurrences, disease events.

💻 Computational Statistics

Monte Carlo Methods ▼

Uses random sampling to solve mathematical problems. Estimate π, evaluate integrals, price financial options, propagate uncertainty.

E[f(X)] ≈ (1/N) Σᵢ f(Xᵢ) as N → ∞ Convergence rate: O(N^{-1/2})
Bootstrap ▼

Resampling technique: draw B samples of size n WITH replacement from your data. Each bootstrap sample gives one estimate. The distribution of estimates approximates the sampling distribution.

95% CI: [θ̂*_{0.025}, θ̂*_{0.975}] where θ̂* are bootstrap estimates
Brilliance: Works for ANY statistic — even when no formula for its standard error exists.
Cross-Validation ▼

Estimates out-of-sample model performance by repeatedly splitting data into training and validation sets. k-fold CV splits data into k equal parts, using each as validation once.

k-fold CV error = (1/k) Σⱼ MSE_j Leave-one-out (LOOCV): k = n
Use: Model selection, tuning regularization parameter λ, comparing algorithms.

📚 Supervised Learning

🌲 Decision Trees & Random Forest ▼

Decision trees partition feature space into regions using greedy recursive splitting. Random forests aggregate B trees trained on bootstrap samples with random feature subsets — dramatically reducing variance.

🚀 Gradient Boosting (XGBoost, LightGBM) ▼

Builds an additive ensemble of weak learners (shallow trees) by fitting each new tree to the residuals of the previous ensemble. Gradient descent in function space.

F_m(x) = F_{m-1}(x) + η·h_m(x) where h_m fits residuals −∂L/∂F_{m-1}
XGBoost & LightGBM dominate structured data competitions. Often outperforms deep learning on tabular data.

Section 14 · Spatial & Functional Statistics

Data Across Space & Functions

🗺️ Spatial Statistics

Geostatistics & Kriging
Interpolates spatially correlated data using the variogram. "Best linear unbiased predictor" at unobserved locations. Used in mining, environmental monitoring, climate science.
Spatial Point Processes
Models random patterns of points in space. Poisson process = complete spatial randomness. Tests for clustering or regularity. Used in ecology, epidemiology, seismology.
Spatial Regression
Handles spatial autocorrelation in regression residuals. Spatial lag models, spatial error models. Moran's I tests for spatial independence.

∫ Functional Data Analysis

Functional Data
Each observation is a function (curve) rather than a scalar or vector. Growth curves, EEG signals, spectrometric data, daily temperature profiles.
Functional PCA (FPCA)
Generalizes PCA to functional data. Decomposes curves into functional principal components capturing main modes of variation.
Functional Regression
Regression where predictor or response (or both) are functions. Scalar-on-function, function-on-scalar, function-on-function regression.
Section 15 · Modern Research Frontiers

The Cutting Edge of Statistics

Active research areas where statistics intersects with modern science, technology, and society.

🔗 Causal Inference Moving beyond correlation ▼

The Fundamental Challenge

Correlation ≠ causation. Causal inference provides tools to estimate causal effects from observational data.

ATE = E[Y(1) − Y(0)] (Average Treatment Effect)

Methods

Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs), Instrumental Variables (IV), Regression Discontinuity, Difference-in-Differences, Propensity Score Matching, Pearl's Do-Calculus.

DAGsCounterfactualsRCTIV
📏 High-Dimensional Statistics When p > n or p ≈ n ▼

The p >> n Problem

Classical statistics assumes n >> p. Modern problems have p >> n (genomics: n=100 patients, p=20,000 genes). OLS fails completely — (XᵀX) is singular.

Solutions

LASSO (sparsity), Ridge, Elastic Net, Compressed Sensing, False Discovery Rate control (Benjamini-Hochberg), Random Matrix Theory.

FDR = E[V/R] = E[False Positives / Total Positives] BH procedure controls FDR at level q
🧬 Statistical Genetics & Genomics Statistics meets biology ▼

Core Problems

GWAS (Genome-Wide Association Studies): test millions of SNPs for disease association. RNA-Seq analysis: differential expression. Population genetics: evolutionary inference.

GWAS: test H₀: SNP unassociated with trait p-value threshold: 5×10⁻⁸ (Bonferroni for 10⁶ tests)

Concept

Train statistical models across decentralized devices (phones, hospitals) without sharing raw data. Privacy preserved via Differential Privacy — mathematical guarantee that individual records cannot be identified.

ε-differential privacy: P(A(D)∈S) ≤ e^ε·P(A(D')∈S) for all neighboring datasets D, D'

🕸️ Network Analysis Statistics on graphs ▼

Statistical Questions

How do networks form? What processes spread on networks? Are two networks from the same distribution? Community detection via stochastic block models.

Methods

Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGM), Stochastic Block Models, Latent Space Models, Network Motif Analysis, Spectral Methods for community detection.

💪 Robust Statistics Resistant to outliers and violations ▼

Motivation

Classical estimators (mean, OLS) are highly sensitive to outliers. Robust methods maintain good performance even when assumptions are violated or outliers are present.

Key Concepts

Breakdown point: Fraction of contamination that causes estimator to fail. Median has BP=50%. Mean has BP=0%.
M-estimators: Generalize MLE with robust loss functions (Huber, Tukey bisquare).

🌡️ Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) Principled uncertainty in complex models ▼

Scope

Quantifying and propagating uncertainty through complex computational models — climate models, physics simulations, engineering systems. Combines statistics with computational science.

Methods

Monte Carlo, Polynomial Chaos Expansion, Gaussian Process Emulators (surrogate models), Bayesian Calibration, Sensitivity Analysis (Sobol indices).

🧩 Mixture Models & Latent Variable Models Hidden structure in data ▼

Framework

Assumes data comes from a mixture of component distributions parameterized by latent (unobserved) variables. EM algorithm for fitting.

Section 17 · Complete Learning Roadmap

Your Path Through Statistics

A structured progression from complete beginner to active researcher. Follow the path — each level builds directly on the last.

1

🌱 Foundation: Math & Probability

Basic arithmetic, algebra, set theory. Introduction to probability — sample spaces, events, Bayes' theorem. Combinatorics. Expected value and variance. Time: 4-8 weeks

2

📊 Descriptive Statistics

Data types and collection. Measures of central tendency and dispersion. Data visualization (histograms, boxplots). Shape measures (skewness, kurtosis). Software: R or Python. Time: 2-4 weeks

3

🎲 Probability Distributions

PMFs, PDFs, CDFs. Major families: Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson, Normal, Exponential. Transformations of random variables. Moment generating functions. Time: 4-6 weeks

4

🔭 Inferential Statistics

Sampling distributions. CLT and LLN. Confidence intervals. Hypothesis testing framework: p-values, Type I/II errors, power. Common tests: z, t, chi-square, ANOVA. Time: 6-8 weeks

5

📈 Regression Analysis

Simple and multiple linear regression. Logistic regression. Model diagnostics. GLMs. Regularization (Ridge, LASSO). Cross-validation. Time: 6-8 weeks

6

🔢 Mathematical Statistics

Estimation theory (MLE, MoM). Sufficient statistics. Cramér-Rao bound. Exponential family. Decision theory. Asymptotic theory. Requires calculus, linear algebra. Time: 8-12 weeks

7

🌊 Multivariate Statistics

Multivariate normal. PCA, Factor Analysis, Discriminant Analysis. Cluster analysis. MANOVA. Dimensionality reduction. Requires linear algebra fluency. Time: 6-8 weeks

8

🧩 Bayesian Statistics

Bayesian framework and philosophy. Prior specification. Conjugate models. MCMC (Metropolis-Hastings, Gibbs). Variational inference. Bayesian computation tools (Stan, PyMC). Time: 8-12 weeks

9

⏳ Specialized Areas

Choose your specialization: Time Series (ARIMA, GARCH), Survival Analysis (Cox model), Spatial Statistics, Nonparametric Methods, or Computational Statistics (Monte Carlo, Bootstrap). Time: 8-16 weeks per area

10

🤖 Statistical Learning & ML

Statistical learning theory. Random forests, boosting. SVMs. Unsupervised learning. Neural networks and deep learning. Bias-variance tradeoff. Time: 8-12 weeks

11

🔬 Advanced Theory

Measure-theoretic probability. Empirical process theory. High-dimensional statistics. Semiparametric theory. Statistical decision theory. This is graduate-level material. Time: 1-2 years

12

🚀 Research & Innovation

Read and contribute to the research literature. Develop new methods. Collaborate across disciplines. Apply methods to novel problems. Engage with the statistical community. Timeline: Ongoing
